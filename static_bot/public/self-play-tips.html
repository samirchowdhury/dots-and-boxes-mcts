5 Tips for Training a Bot with Self-Play

Placeholder intro: I thought this would be a small AlphaZero-style experiment. Then an old JavaScript Dots-and-Boxes bot kept finding ways to make the training loop look silly.

1. Start with boring baselines

Placeholder text: before self-play gets interesting, make random play, legal move generation, scoring, and game termination painfully correct. Most mysterious training failures are less mysterious after the rules are checked against fixtures and simple baselines.

2. Keep an external opponent around

Placeholder text: self-play can produce agents that are excellent inside their own training distribution and strangely brittle outside it. A fixed external bot is useful because it does not politely share your agent's blind spots.

3. Treat every breakthrough as provisional

Placeholder text: a checkpoint that looks strong today can be surpassed, exploited, or forgotten tomorrow. Save checkpoints, run repeatable evaluations, and expect progress to look bumpier than a single headline number suggests.

4. More search is not always better

Placeholder text: MCTS gives the bot more time to think, but a miscalibrated policy or value estimate can still steer that thinking into trouble. One of the stranger lessons here was that the bot sometimes performed better when it resisted the urge to overthink.

5. Self-play finds exploits before wisdom

Placeholder text: self-play is powerful because it creates its own curriculum, but that curriculum is only as good as the pressure applied to it. The agent learns the game you actually evaluate, not the tidy story you hoped it would learn.
