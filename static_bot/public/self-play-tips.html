5 Tips For Training Your First AlphaZero Bot

These were the gaps I needed to fill while building my first AlphaZero (AZ)-style bot to play a game—specifically, Dots-and-Boxes—using Codex and GPT-5.5.

1. Check if your game is suitable for Monte Carlo Tree Search

Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is a core ingredient of AlphaZero and related algorithms. This is a good fit for perfect information games where all players see the entire history and state of the game. Dots-and-Boxes is one example. Card games where a player cannot see other players' hands are imperfect information games, and may need extra machinery.

2. Port your MCTS to a compiled language as soon as you can

Learning from self-play is extremely iteration-hungry. I knew this—but didn't really know it—until I read the breakdown in the ELF OpenGo paper's appendix about the thousands of TPUs/GPUs (and who knows how much CPU) used for generating self-play games. One of the best things that independent devs can do for their laptop bots is to move MCTS search out of Python and into a compiled language as quickly as we can.

3. Read the algorithm very carefully, and search for external resources

Authors may leave out implementation details despite their best efforts at providing completeness. Sometimes this is necessary to accommodate paper length limits (Science and Nature have extremely tight limits). The Appendix and Supplementary Materials are usually your friends. The AZ paper's Supplementary Materials contain a pseudocode.py file that I found very useful. The ELF OpenGo paper provided numerous useful details for both AZ and AlphaGo Zero (AGZ) in addition to its own implementation details. While learning, I found it very useful to leverage these external resources to write out the algorithm carefully (but not before I got burned by a poor understanding of the replay buffer that led to a painful exploit loop).

4. Keep external evals

Dots-and-Boxes is well-studied mathematically and has many implementations available online. I never trained against any of them, but I did use one of these implementations to test my bot periodically. Beating an external bot is an excellent motivator in addition to being a good sanity check! Watch out for the Terms of Service though; the first external bot I played against had a clause in its ToS against automated play, and I pulled my code as soon as I realized this. The bot at dotsandboxes.org turned out to be much better for my use case, as the game engine could be run client-side without spamming anyone's personal server. If your game has external browser-based implementations, note that Codex is excellent at wiring up your bot to play in a browser.

5. Expect regressions

Playing against an external bot with a deterministic strategy helped me realize that self-play will not automatically improve overall strength, and that regressions are not surprising. Self-play can create a situation where A loses to B, B loses to C, but A is able to exploit C. I later realized that this was a well-known phenomenon; the AlphaStar paper described this issue and pointed to mitigation techniques (although I have yet to try them).
